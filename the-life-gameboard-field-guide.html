The Life Gameboard Field Guide

Wild Womaniya Studio June 16, 2026

The Life Gameboard

Stop Managing Your Life. Start Playing It.

Introduction

Many people know exactly what they want to do.

They have notebooks full of ideas. Bookmarks full of resources. Courses waiting to be completed.

The problem isn’t a lack of ambition.

The problem is that most people are trying to manage life like a project.

Life isn’t a project.

It’s a gameboard.

Projects come and go.

Skills increase.

New opportunities appear.

Unexpected challenges arrive.

The people who keep moving aren’t necessarily more disciplined.

They’re better at seeing progress.

This guide will show you how to turn your life into a visible gameboard so that progress becomes easier to see, easier to track, and easier to continue.

Who This Is For

This guide is for:

  • People with too many interests
  • Creatives managing multiple projects
  • People who start strong but lose momentum
  • Anyone who struggles to see their own progress
  • Builders who want a simple system instead of another productivity method

Who This Is Not For

This guide is not for:

  • People looking for a rigid planning system
  • People who enjoy tracking every minute of their day
  • People who want perfection before action
  • People who need certainty before starting

How This Guide Will Help

By the end of this guide, you will:

  • See your life differently
  • Make progress visible
  • Create momentum through small wins
  • Track growth without complicated systems
  • Build a habit of finishing things

Most importantly, you’ll stop feeling like you’re standing still when you’re actually moving.

Core Observation

Most people only count outcomes.

They count:

  • Money earned
  • Followers gained
  • Projects completed
  • Goals achieved

But life is mostly built before the outcome appears.

The skill is developed before the opportunity arrives.

The confidence is built before the presentation.

The habit is established before the transformation.

If you only count outcomes, you’ll constantly feel behind.

If you count progress, you’ll always have evidence that you’re moving.

Main Framework

Think of your life as a gameboard made up of four elements:

Quests

A quest is anything you’re currently working toward.

Examples:

  • Publish a field guide
  • Learn watercolor painting
  • Build a community kitchen project
  • Organize your digital files

A quest should be specific enough to complete.

Not:

“Improve my life.”

Instead:

“Complete my first field guide.”

Powerups

Powerups are things that make future quests easier.

Examples:

  • Learning Canva
  • Learning Notion
  • Public speaking
  • Better sleep habits
  • Writing consistently

Powerups matter because they continue helping long after a quest is completed.

Checkpoints

Checkpoints are visible markers of progress.

Examples:

  • First draft completed
  • First customer served
  • First event organized
  • First article published

Checkpoints prove movement.

They prevent discouragement.

Boss Battles

Boss battles are the challenges that keep showing up.

Examples:

  • Procrastination
  • Fear of criticism
  • Perfectionism
  • Starting too many projects
  • Difficulty finishing

The goal isn’t to eliminate every boss.

The goal is to get better at facing them.

Field Observations

Observation 1

People underestimate small wins.

Writing one page feels insignificant.

Writing one page every day creates a book.

Observation 2

People often quit because they cannot see progress.

The progress exists.

The tracking doesn’t.

Observation 3

Skills are often more valuable than outcomes.

A failed project can still leave behind:

  • Better writing
  • Better design
  • Better communication
  • Better decision-making

Those are permanent powerups.

Observation 4

Completion changes identity.

Every completed quest teaches your brain:

“I am someone who finishes things.”

That belief becomes a powerup too.

Practical Exercise

Create your first Life Gameboard.

Take a blank page.

Create four sections.

Active Quests

List 3–5 things you’re currently trying to complete.

Example:

  • Publish a field guide
  • Finish organizing photos
  • Learn basic watercolor

Current Powerups

List skills you’re actively building.

Example:

  • Writing
  • Design
  • Project management

Recent Checkpoints

List five things you’ve completed recently.

Even small things count.

Example:

  • Sent an important email
  • Finished a draft
  • Cleaned workspace
  • Published one post
  • Completed one lesson

Boss Battles

List recurring obstacles.

Example:

  • Perfectionism
  • Distraction
  • Overcommitting

You now have a visible gameboard.

Quick Start Version

If you only have five minutes:

  1. Write down one active quest.
  2. Write down three powerups you’re building.
  3. Write down five recent wins.
  4. Write down one boss battle.
  5. Review once a week.

That’s enough to begin.

Checklist

Before finishing this guide:

  • I identified at least one active quest.
  • I identified at least three powerups.
  • I listed recent checkpoints.
  • I identified recurring boss battles.
  • I created a simple gameboard.
  • I scheduled a weekly review.

Next Step

Create a single page in your notebook, Notion workspace, or journal called:

Life Gameboard

Update it once a week.

Do not redesign it.

Do not optimize it.

Do not turn it into a productivity system.

Use it.

The goal is visibility, not complexity.

Final Reflection

Most people are waiting for a breakthrough.

A breakthrough is usually a collection of small steps that finally became visible.

You don’t need a new life.

You need a better way to see the life you’re already building.

Treat your days like quests.

Collect powerups.

Celebrate checkpoints.

Face boss battles.

Then continue.

One tiny step at a time.
