The Publishing as Market Research Field Guide

Wild Womaniya Studio June 13, 2026

Publishing as Market Research

How To Discover What People Want By Publishing Useful Things

Many people treat publishing as the final step.

The guide gets written.

The product gets built.

The website goes live.

The launch begins.

Only then does feedback arrive.

Sometimes the feedback is encouraging.

Sometimes it reveals a problem.

The wrong topic.

The wrong audience.

The wrong solution.

The wrong assumptions.

That can be an expensive way to learn.

There is a simpler approach.

Use publishing as market research.

The Traditional Approach

The traditional approach often looks like this:

Research.

Plan.

Build.

Launch.

Hope.

The challenge is obvious.

Months of work can be invested before discovering whether anyone actually cares.

A Different Approach

Instead:

Publish.

Learn.

Adapt.

Repeat.

Small publications create information.

That information becomes more valuable than assumptions.

What Counts As Publishing?

Publishing does not require:

  • a large audience
  • a website
  • a newsletter
  • a course
  • a business

Publishing simply means sharing something useful.

Examples:

  • A Field Guide
  • A checklist
  • A worksheet
  • A framework
  • A resource list
  • A short article

The goal is not scale.

The goal is learning.

Every Publication Answers Questions

A useful publication can reveal:

  • what people care about
  • what people ignore
  • what people misunderstand
  • what people struggle with
  • what people want more of

That information is difficult to discover in theory.

It becomes obvious in practice.

The BE → DO → HAVE Example

Imagine a short guide is published about BE → DO → HAVE.

Some readers enjoy it.

Some readers ignore it.

Some readers reply.

A pattern appears.

Many responses mention the same thing:

“I know what to do. I just don’t do it.”

That is valuable information.

A stronger publication idea has just appeared.

Not because of brainstorming.

Because readers revealed it.

Small Publications Create Fast Feedback

Large projects create slow feedback.

Small projects create fast feedback.

A three-page guide can teach more than three months of planning.

Not because it is better.

Because it reaches reality sooner.

Reality is an excellent teacher.

Questions Are More Valuable Than Praise

Many creators look for approval.

A more useful goal is understanding.

Instead of asking:

“Did you like it?”

Ask:

  • What part landed hardest?
  • What are you struggling with right now?
  • What would you like more help with?

These questions create better information.

Patterns Matter

One response is interesting.

Five responses are encouraging.

Twenty responses become useful.

Look for repetition.

Repeated questions.

Repeated frustrations.

Repeated language.

Repeated desires.

Patterns reveal opportunities.

Publishing From Zero Audience

This approach works particularly well for people starting from scratch.

No audience?

No problem.

Start small.

Share with:

  • friends
  • colleagues
  • communities
  • existing contacts

The goal is not reach.

The goal is response.

Ten thoughtful readers can teach more than a thousand passive followers.

The Publishing Research Loop

Step 1:

Create something useful.

Step 2:

Publish it.

Step 3:

Ask questions.

Step 4:

Collect responses.

Step 5:

Look for patterns.

Step 6:

Create the next publication based on what was learned.

Then repeat.

Why This Works

Most people try to discover demand before creating anything.

Publishing creates evidence.

Evidence reduces guessing.

Less guessing leads to better decisions.

Better decisions create more useful work.

The First Experiment

Choose one idea.

Create the smallest useful version.

Publish it.

Ask questions.

Collect responses.

Study patterns.

Then decide what to build next.

That is enough.

Reflection Questions

  • What publication could be created this week?
  • What assumptions currently exist about readers?
  • What information would be most useful right now?
  • What could be learned by publishing instead of planning?

Final Thought

Publishing is not just a way to share ideas.

It is a way to discover them.

Every useful publication teaches something.

The question is not:

“What should be built next?”

The question is:

“What is the next thing reality can teach?”
