The Borrow A Voice Until You Find Your Own Field Guide

Wild Womaniya Studio June 17, 2026

Borrow A Voice Until You Find Your Own

Use Training Wheels. Just Don’t Build A House On Them.

Introduction

One of the most frustrating pieces of creative advice is:

“Just be yourself.”

Most beginners have no idea what that means.

They know what they like.

They know who they admire.

They know certain creators make them feel something.

But they don’t yet know how to translate that into their own work.

This is normal.

Musicians learn by playing other people’s songs.

Artists learn by copying masterworks.

Chefs learn by recreating existing recipes.

Writers are no different.

The problem is not borrowing a voice.

The problem is never moving beyond it.

This guide will show you how to use admired voices as training wheels while slowly discovering your own.

Who This Is For

This guide is for:

  • Beginning writers
  • Creators finding their style
  • Educators building a teaching voice
  • Content creators
  • Artists who feel stuck
  • Anyone who says, “I don’t know how I want to sound.”

Who This Is Not For

This guide is not for:

  • People looking to copy someone permanently
  • People trying to build a fake identity
  • People seeking shortcuts to mastery
  • People unwilling to practice

How This Guide Will Help

By the end of this guide, you will:

  • Understand how creative borrowing works
  • Learn from admired creators without becoming a clone
  • Build confidence faster
  • Identify what genuinely resonates with you
  • Develop a voice that feels natural

Core Observation

Most creators don’t discover their voice in isolation.

They discover it through influence.

Influence is not failure.

Influence is part of learning.

The danger comes when influence becomes imitation.

Borrowing teaches.

Copying limits.

The goal is not to sound exactly like someone else.

The goal is to understand what attracts you to their work.

Main Framework

Stage 1: Identify Your Creative Teachers

Choose three to five people whose work you admire.

They can be:

  • Writers
  • Artists
  • Filmmakers
  • Educators
  • Podcasters
  • Historical figures
  • Fictional characters

Examples:

You may admire someone for:

  • Clarity
  • Warmth
  • Humor
  • Precision
  • Curiosity
  • Courage

Write their names down.

Stage 2: Study The Attraction

Ask:

Why do I like this person’s work?

Be specific.

Not:

“They’re amazing.”

Instead:

  • They explain complex ideas simply.
  • They tell memorable stories.
  • They ask good questions.
  • They are direct.
  • They are playful.

You’re looking for ingredients.

Not personalities.

Stage 3: Experiment

Take a simple topic.

Write about it three different ways.

Example:

Explain how to make tea.

Write it:

  • As Creator A
  • As Creator B
  • As Creator C

Notice what feels natural.

Notice what feels forced.

Pay attention to your energy.

Stage 4: Extract The Ingredients

After experimenting, identify:

What do I want to keep?

Examples:

  • Clarity from one creator
  • Warmth from another
  • Structure from another
  • Curiosity from another

You are building a toolkit.

Not a costume.

Stage 5: Return To Yourself

Now write the same topic again.

Without consciously copying anyone.

Use what you’ve learned.

Your voice will begin appearing naturally.

Field Observations

Observation 1

Many creators discover their voice accidentally.

They create enough work that patterns emerge.

Observation 2

People often admire creators for different reasons than they think.

What attracts you may be structure, not personality.

Observation 3

Voice develops faster through experimentation than through analysis.

Trying teaches more than theorizing.

Observation 4

Your voice is often a combination of influences.

That’s normal.

Every creator is part of a larger conversation.

Observation 5

The goal isn’t originality.

The goal is honesty.

Originality often arrives later.

Practical Exercise

Create an Influence Map.

Choose three creators you admire.

For each creator answer:

What do I admire?

Examples:

  • Simplicity
  • Depth
  • Humor
  • Precision
  • Storytelling

What do I not want?

Examples:

  • Excess complexity
  • Formal language
  • Long introductions

Learning what doesn’t fit is just as useful.

What can I borrow?

Choose one specific element.

Examples:

  • Short sentences
  • Better examples
  • Stronger structure
  • Clearer explanations

What feels natural?

Pay attention to ease.

What feels effortless is often a clue.

Quick Start Version

If you only have ten minutes:

  1. Pick three creators you admire.
  2. Write down why.
  3. Borrow one element from each.
  4. Create something.
  5. Notice what feels natural.

Done.

Checklist

Before finishing this guide:

  • I identified creators I admire.
  • I identified specific qualities I admire.
  • I experimented with different styles.
  • I extracted useful ingredients.
  • I identified what feels natural.
  • I created something in my own voice.

Next Step

Create a page called:

Influence Map

Whenever you discover a creator you admire, add them.

Don’t collect people.

Collect lessons.

Over time you’ll notice something interesting.

The qualities you admire most often reveal the creator you’re becoming.

Final Reflection

Nobody starts with a fully formed voice.

Every creator begins as a student.

Every artist borrows.

Every writer imitates.

Every builder learns from someone.

The mistake isn’t borrowing.

The mistake is never graduating.

Use other voices as bridges.

Cross them.

Learn from them.

Thank them.

Then keep walking.

Your own voice is waiting on the other side.
