Three Questions for Your Audience

A Simple Reader Research System For Creators, Writers, Educators, and Builders

Many people struggle with audience building because they are trying to answer a difficult question:

What do people want?

The usual response is to guess.

Research trends.

Study competitors.

Analyze algorithms.

Consume marketing advice.

None of those things are necessarily wrong.

But there is a simpler approach.

Ask people.

Then listen carefully.

Over time, I discovered that three questions repeatedly produce better insights than long surveys, complicated forms, or audience avatars.

These questions are simple.

But they reveal far more than most people expect.

Why Most Audience Research Fails

Many creators ask questions that are too broad.

Examples:

  • What content do you want?
  • What should I create next?
  • What are your goals?

The answers are often vague.

People struggle to describe solutions.

They are much better at describing problems.

The goal is not to ask people what product they want.

The goal is to understand what they are experiencing.

Question One

What Part Landed Hardest?

This is one of the most useful questions you can ask after someone reads your work.

It reveals:

  • what resonated
  • what felt true
  • what created emotional connection
  • what readers remember

Pay attention to repeated answers.

If twenty people highlight the same paragraph, chapter, or idea, that is important.

The audience is showing you where the energy is.

Question Two

What Are You Struggling With Right Now?

This question reveals the real problem.

Not the problem you think people have.

The problem they actually experience.

Readers often tell you:

  • what frustrates them
  • what confuses them
  • what keeps repeating
  • what they feel stuck on

These answers are gold.

Many future publications can emerge from a single recurring struggle.

Question Three

If I Expanded One Section Into A Full Handbook, Which Section Should It Be?

This question does something powerful.

It asks readers to vote.

Not with money.

Not with likes.

With attention.

People reveal what they want more of.

If the same topic keeps appearing, you have found a strong candidate for expansion.

Why These Questions Work

Together, they answer three different things.

Question One reveals:

What resonated?

Question Two reveals:

What hurts?

Question Three reveals:

What deserves expansion?

When combined, they create direction.

The Difference Between Opinions And Patterns

One response is interesting.

Five responses are encouraging.

Twenty responses are data.

Do not build your next publication based on one person’s opinion.

Look for repetition.

Patterns matter more than individual feedback.

When the same issue appears repeatedly, pay attention.

Building A Reader-Led Publishing Strategy

Many creators decide what to build first.

Then search for an audience.

This approach reverses the process.

You publish.

You listen.

You learn.

You build.

Readers become collaborators.

Not because they are creating the publication.

But because their experiences help shape it.

What To Do With The Answers

Create a simple document.

Each time someone replies:

Record:

  • their answer
  • their struggle
  • their suggested expansion

After enough responses, patterns become obvious.

You will begin noticing:

  • recurring frustrations
  • recurring goals
  • recurring language
  • recurring questions

Those patterns become future publications.

Bonus Question

If appropriate, you can also ask:

Tell Me About Yourself

Optional information:

  • Name
  • Age
  • Location
  • What you do
  • Website or portfolio

This helps you understand who is reading your work.

Not just what they are saying.

Over time, you’ll begin seeing who your publications naturally attract.

Your First Reader Research Experiment

Publish something useful.

Ask these three questions.

Collect responses.

Look for patterns.

Then create your next publication based on what you learn.

Do this repeatedly.

Over time, you’ll stop guessing.

Reflection Questions

  • What assumptions am I making about my audience?
  • What evidence do I actually have?
  • What patterns have I noticed recently?
  • What question could I ask that would help me create something more useful?

Final Thought

You do not need a large audience to understand people.

You need curiosity.

You need attention.

And you need the willingness to listen.

Sometimes three thoughtful questions can teach you more than months of speculation.
