The Validation Loop Field Guide

Wild Womaniya Studio June 12, 2026

The Validation Loop

How to Discover What People Actually Want Before You Build It

Many creators spend months building things nobody asked for.

Courses.

Books.

Products.

Services.

Entire businesses.

Then they launch.

And hear silence.

Not because the work was bad.

Because they built before they learned.

There is a simpler approach.

I call it:

The Validation Loop.

Instead of guessing what people want, you create something small, learn from real people, and use that information to decide what to build next.

The Problem With Guessing

Most people begin with assumptions.

They assume:

  • what people need
  • what people will pay for
  • what problem matters most
  • what language resonates

Sometimes they’re right.

Often they’re not.

The challenge is that assumptions feel like knowledge.

Until they meet reality.

What Validation Actually Means

Validation is not approval.

Validation is information.

You’re not asking:

“Do people like me?”

You’re asking:

“Does this problem matter?”

Those are very different questions.

A useful publication can fail to get attention because the topic is wrong.

A rough publication can succeed because the problem is important.

Focus on the problem.

Not your ego.

The Validation Loop

The process is simple:

Create

↓

Share

↓

Collect Feedback

↓

Find Patterns

↓

Build Next

Then repeat.

Again and again.

Every cycle teaches you something.

Step 1: Create Something Small

Do not start with a course.

Do not start with a membership.

Do not start with a complete ecosystem.

Start with something small enough to finish.

Examples:

  • A Field Guide
  • A checklist
  • A framework
  • A resource list
  • A worksheet
  • A short tutorial

Your goal is not perfection.

Your goal is publication.

Step 2: Share It

Share it where you already are.

Not where you think you should be.

Examples:

  • WhatsApp
  • Facebook
  • LinkedIn
  • Existing contacts
  • Friends
  • Communities you already belong to

You do not need a large audience.

You need a few real people.

Step 3: Collect Feedback

Ask specific questions.

Bad question:

“Did you like it?”

Good questions:

  • What part landed hardest?
  • What are you struggling with right now?
  • What would you like more help with?

Specific questions produce useful answers.

Step 4: Look For Patterns

One response is interesting.

Ten responses are data.

Twenty responses are direction.

Look for repetition.

What problems appear again and again?

What words do people use?

What frustrations keep showing up?

Patterns matter more than individual opinions.

Step 5: Build The Next Thing

Now create something based on what you learned.

Not what you guessed.

Not what you assumed.

Not what sounded clever.

What people actually asked for.

This dramatically increases the chances that your next publication will be useful.

Why This Works

Because every publication becomes research.

Instead of asking:

“What should I build?”

You begin asking:

“What am I learning?”

This removes pressure.

Publishing becomes experimentation.

Not performance.

A Real Example

Imagine you publish a guide about consistency.

Twenty people write back.

Fifteen mention the same problem:

“I know what to do. I just don’t do it.”

That is valuable information.

You now have evidence.

Your next publication could focus entirely on that problem.

The audience helped create it.

The Hidden Benefit

The Validation Loop doesn’t just help you discover products.

It helps you discover people.

You begin learning:

  • who reads your work
  • what they care about
  • what language they use
  • what challenges they face
  • what solutions they value

Over time, an audience emerges naturally.

Not because you chased one.

Because you listened.

Your First Validation Experiment

Choose one idea.

Create the smallest useful version of it.

Share it.

Ask three questions.

Collect responses.

Look for patterns.

Then decide what to create next.

Do not skip the feedback step.

That is where the real value lives.

Reflection Questions

  • What am I currently assuming about my audience?
  • What evidence do I actually have?
  • What is the smallest version of my idea I could publish?
  • What feedback would help me most right now?

Final Thought

Many people treat publishing as the final step.

It works better as the first step.

Publish.

Learn.

Adapt.

Repeat.

The more cycles you complete, the less guessing you need to do.

And the less guessing you do, the more useful your work becomes.
