The Personal Universe vs Public Projects Field Guide

Wild Womaniya Studio June 16, 2026

Personal Universe vs Public Projects

How To Organize Your Work Without Splitting Yourself Apart

Introduction

Many people struggle to explain what they do.

Not because they lack clarity.

Because they do many things.

They’re artists and organizers.

Writers and facilitators.

Community builders and entrepreneurs.

Creators often feel pressure to simplify themselves into a single label.

Pick one thing.

Choose one niche.

Stay in one lane.

But real life rarely works that way.

The challenge isn’t having multiple interests.

The challenge is organizing them in a way that makes sense.

This guide will help you separate your Personal Universe from your Public Projects so your work becomes easier to understand, easier to communicate, and easier to grow.

Who This Is For

This guide is for:

  • Multi-passionate creators
  • Artists with several ongoing projects
  • Community builders
  • Founders with multiple initiatives
  • People who struggle to explain what they do

Who This Is Not For

This guide is not for:

  • People who only work on one thing
  • People looking for branding tricks
  • People trying to manufacture an identity
  • People who want a shortcut instead of clarity

How This Guide Will Help

By the end of this guide, you will:

  • Understand the difference between a universe and a project
  • Organize multiple interests without confusion
  • Present your work more clearly
  • Reduce the feeling of being scattered
  • Create a structure that can grow over time

Core Observation

Most people organize their identity around projects.

This creates a problem.

Projects end.

Businesses change.

Roles evolve.

Communities shift.

When your identity is attached to a project, every change feels like a crisis.

A stronger approach is to build around a Personal Universe.

Projects become expressions of that universe.

Not replacements for it.

Main Framework

Part 1: Your Personal Universe

Your Personal Universe is the world you are building.

It contains:

  • Your values
  • Your interests
  • Your questions
  • Your philosophies
  • Your way of seeing the world

This is the layer that remains consistent even when projects change.

Examples:

A person may care deeply about:

  • Creativity
  • Simplicity
  • Community
  • Learning by doing

Those themes become part of their universe.

Part 2: Your Public Projects

Projects are how your universe becomes visible.

Projects are temporary.

They are expressions.

Not identities.

Examples:

A creator’s universe may express itself through:

  • A community initiative
  • A podcast
  • A book
  • A workshop
  • A game
  • A nonprofit

Different projects.

Same underlying universe.

Part 3: The Connection Layer

This is where many people get stuck.

They create projects.

But never explain how those projects connect.

The result looks random.

The connection layer explains:

Why these projects exist.

What links them together.

What larger story they belong to.

Without this layer, people see separate projects.

With this layer, people see a coherent body of work.

Field Observations

Observation 1

People often think they have too many interests.

The real problem is usually missing structure.

Observation 2

Projects are easier to start than universes.

That’s why many people have lots of projects but no clear identity.

Observation 3

A strong universe creates flexibility.

Projects can evolve without creating confusion.

Observation 4

The most memorable creators rarely build isolated projects.

They build worlds.

People remember the world.

Not individual tasks completed inside it.

Practical Exercise

Create your Universe Map.

Take a blank page.

Draw three sections.

Section 1: Core Themes

List the ideas that repeatedly appear in your life.

Examples:

  • Creativity
  • Education
  • Community
  • Simplicity
  • Adventure
  • Sustainability

Choose five.

These become the foundations of your universe.

Section 2: Current Projects

List every active project.

Examples:

  • Newsletter
  • Community initiative
  • Art practice
  • Local volunteering
  • Digital product

Don’t organize them yet.

Just list them.

Section 3: Connections

For each project ask:

Which core theme does this express?

You will begin noticing patterns.

Those patterns are your universe.

Quick Start Version

If you only have ten minutes:

  1. Write down five themes that matter most to you.
  2. List all active projects.
  3. Connect each project to a theme.
  4. Look for overlaps.
  5. Write one sentence describing your universe.

Done.

Checklist

Before finishing this guide:

  • I identified my core themes.
  • I listed my active projects.
  • I connected projects to themes.
  • I identified the common thread.
  • I wrote a description of my universe.
  • I stopped defining myself by individual projects.

Next Step

Create two pages.

Page One:

Personal Universe

Page Two:

Public Projects

Whenever a new project appears, ask how it connects to your larger universe.

Instead of creating projects that stand alone, connect them to the themes, values, and ideas that matter most to you.

Use your universe as the foundation.

Let your projects grow naturally from that foundation.

Final Reflection

A tree is not defined by a single branch.

A river is not defined by a single bend.

A life is not defined by a single project.

Projects are visible.

The universe beneath them is not.

Yet the invisible structure is what gives everything meaning.

Build the universe.

Let the projects come and go.

The universe will remain.
