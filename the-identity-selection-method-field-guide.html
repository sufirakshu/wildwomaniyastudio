The Identity Selection Method

How To Choose An Identity That Actually Changes Something

Many people begin identity work with a question like:

Who do I want to be?

It sounds useful.

But it often creates confusion.

The answers tend to be vague.

  • Confident
  • Successful
  • Wealthy
  • Productive
  • Aligned

The problem is that these identities are difficult to use.

What does “be confident” actually look like on a Tuesday afternoon?

What does “be successful” actually look like today?

A more useful question is:

What identity would naturally create the outcome I’m looking for?

Why Most Identity Work Fails

Many identities sound good.

Few are practical.

Examples:

“I am abundant.”

“I am successful.”

“I am powerful.”

Perhaps.

But what happens next?

What action follows?

What changes today?

If an identity doesn’t influence behaviour, it is difficult to build.

The Observable Identity Rule

A simple rule:

Choose identities that can be observed.

Someone watching your actions should be able to recognise the identity.

Examples:

Instead of:

“I am creative.”

Try:

“I am someone who creates.”

Instead of:

“I am healthy.”

Try:

“I am someone who moves my body daily.”

Instead of:

“I am organised.”

Try:

“I am someone who puts things away after using them.”

The more observable the identity, the easier it becomes to practice.

Start With The Outcome

Most people already know what they want.

Examples:

  • Write a book
  • Build a YouTube channel
  • Declutter a home
  • Improve fitness
  • Create a body of work

Start there.

Write down the outcome.

Then ask:

Who naturally creates this result?

Work Backwards

Example:

Outcome:

Publish useful resources.

Question:

Who naturally publishes useful resources?

Possible identity:

I am someone who ships useful work consistently.

Now the path becomes clearer.

Example One: Wild Womaniya

Desired outcome:

Create practical resources that people can actually use.

Possible identity:

I am someone who experiments, documents, and publishes.

Natural actions:

  • Write Field Guides.
  • Test ideas.
  • Share observations.
  • Publish consistently.

Notice what happened.

The identity creates the actions.

The actions create the outcome.

Example Two: The Ecstatic Alchemist

Desired outcome:

Help people close the gap between knowing and doing.

Possible identity:

I am someone who practices what I teach.

Natural actions:

  • Run small experiments.
  • Test ideas personally.
  • Document observations.
  • Share practical lessons.

Again, the identity points toward behaviour.

Example Three: Decluttering

Desired outcome:

A calmer, less cluttered home.

Possible identity:

I am someone who makes small decluttering decisions daily.

Natural actions:

  • Remove one item.
  • Repair something.
  • Put things away.
  • Donate unused items.

The identity makes daily decisions easier.

Avoid Fantasy Identities

Some identities feel exciting.

But they are too far away from present reality.

Examples:

  • Bestselling author
  • Millionaire entrepreneur
  • World-class expert

These are outcomes disguised as identities.

They don’t tell you what to do today.

Focus on identities that can be practiced immediately.

Choose The Smallest Useful Identity

Smaller identities are easier to build.

Instead of:

“I am a successful creator.”

Try:

“I am someone who publishes.”

Instead of:

“I am financially free.”

Try:

“I am someone who manages money carefully.”

Small identities survive real life.

The Identity Test

Before choosing an identity, ask:

  1. Is it observable?

  2. Does it suggest clear actions?

  3. Can it be practiced today?

  4. Would repeating those actions create the outcome I want?

If the answer is yes, the identity is probably useful.

The Identity Selection Exercise

Step 1

Write down one outcome you want.

Step 2

Ask:

Who naturally creates this outcome?

Step 3

Turn the answer into an identity.

I am someone who:

Step 4

List three actions this person naturally does.

Step 5

Choose one action to practice this week.

Reflection Questions

  • Am I choosing identities or outcomes?
  • Can my identity be observed?
  • What action naturally follows from this identity?
  • What identity would make my next goal easier?

Final Thought

The goal is not to choose the most impressive identity.

The goal is to choose the most useful one.

A useful identity changes behaviour.

Changed behaviour creates different results.

Start with the outcome.

Work backwards.

Choose the identity.

Practice the actions.

Let the results follow.
