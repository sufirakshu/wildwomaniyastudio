The 'One Problem, One Method, One Audience' Field Guide

Wild Womaniya Studio June 13, 2026

One Problem, One Method, One Audience

Why Audience Usually Comes Last

One of the most common pieces of advice for creators is:

“Find your audience.”

Sounds reasonable.

Yet many people get stuck there.

Questions appear immediately:

  • Who are they?
  • How old are they?
  • What do they do?
  • What are their interests?
  • What do they want?

Hours disappear.

Days disappear.

Sometimes months disappear.

Meanwhile, nothing gets created.

A simpler approach is:

Start with a problem.

Develop a method.

Discover the audience.

Start With A Problem

Most useful work begins with a problem.

Something frustrating.

Something recurring.

Something difficult to ignore.

A question that keeps appearing.

A challenge that keeps showing up.

The problem becomes the starting point.

Not demographics.

Not audience avatars.

Not market segments.

A real problem.

Then Develop A Method

Once a problem is visible, experimentation begins.

Different approaches get tested.

Some work.

Some don’t.

Patterns start appearing.

Eventually a method emerges.

Not because it was planned from the beginning.

Because repeated experiments revealed what worked.

The method becomes the bridge between the problem and the solution.

Then Discover The Audience

Once the work is shared, something interesting happens.

People start responding.

Questions appear.

Emails arrive.

Conversations begin.

Certain people recognise themselves in the problem.

Those people become the audience.

The audience was not invented.

It was discovered.

Example One: Wild Womaniya

The starting point was not an audience.

The starting point was an observation.

Many people want practical change.

Many resources provide inspiration.

Far fewer provide implementation.

So the experiment became:

What happens when practical resources become the focus?

Field Guides.

Handbooks.

Experiments.

Simple systems.

The audience appeared afterwards.

People who enjoy learning by doing started paying attention.

People who prefer practical action over endless inspiration started paying attention.

The work attracted them.

Example Two: The Ecstatic Alchemist

The starting point was a question.

Why do people know what to do and still struggle to do it?

That question appeared again and again.

A method slowly emerged.

BE.

DO.

HAVE.

Identity shifts.

Small experiments.

Observable actions.

The audience appeared later.

People caught between knowing and doing recognised the problem immediately.

Many of them were interested in personal growth.

Many were interested in spirituality.

Many were simply tired of staying stuck.

The audience emerged from the problem.

Example Three: Marie Kondo

The starting point was clutter.

Homes felt crowded.

Possessions felt overwhelming.

Decision-making felt exhausting.

A method emerged:

  • Keep what sparks joy.
  • Tidy by category.
  • Finish completely.

People struggling with clutter recognised themselves in the problem.

The audience followed naturally.

Why This Matters

Starting with an audience often leads to guessing.

Starting with a problem leads to observation.

Observation leads to experimentation.

Experimentation leads to methods.

Useful methods attract people.

Those people become the audience.

A Simpler Way To Begin

Instead of asking:

Who is my audience?

Try asking:

What problem keeps showing up?

Then ask:

What am I testing?

Then ask:

Who seems interested whenever this topic comes up?

That is often enough to begin.

The One Problem Exercise

Step 1

Write down one problem that keeps appearing.

Not ten.

One.

Step 2

Write down what has already been tested.

Step 3

Write down what seems to be working.

Step 4

Write down who seems most interested.

Reflection Questions

  • What problem keeps showing up?
  • What experiments have already been run?
  • What method is beginning to emerge?
  • Who seems interested in the results?

Final Thought

Many people wait until they have identified the perfect audience.

Useful work rarely begins that way.

A problem appears.

A method emerges.

The work gets shared.

The audience reveals itself.

One problem.

One method.

One audience.

In that order.

About The Ecstatic Alchemist

The Ecstatic Alchemist is the creative practice of Rakshitha Prakash—a space for exploring creativity, personal growth, simple living, and taking small steps toward meaningful change.

At its heart is a simple question:

What becomes possible when we stop waiting and start doing?
