The Simplest Consistency System Field Guide

Wild Womaniya Studio June 12, 2026

The Simplest Consistency System

Why Most Consistency Problems Are Actually Complexity Problems

People often say they want to be more consistent.

Consistent with exercise.

Consistent with writing.

Consistent with content creation.

Consistent with decluttering.

Consistent with almost anything.

The usual assumption is:

More discipline is needed.

Sometimes that’s true.

More often, the problem is something else.

The system is too complicated.

What Usually Happens

A new goal appears.

Excitement follows.

A detailed plan gets created.

New habits are added.

Tracking systems appear.

Schedules get redesigned.

Everything feels promising.

For a few days.

Then real life arrives.

Work gets busy.

Energy drops.

Something unexpected happens.

The system falls apart.

Not because the person failed.

Because the system required perfect conditions.

Consistency Needs To Survive Ordinary Life

A useful system should survive:

  • busy weeks
  • low-energy days
  • interruptions
  • travel
  • unexpected problems
  • imperfect moods

If a system only works when everything goes well, it is fragile.

Most lives are not built around perfect conditions.

The Simplest System

Choose:

One identity.

One action.

One time.

Then repeat.

That is the entire system.

Start With The Identity

Most people start with the action.

A simpler approach is to start one step earlier.

Ask:

Who naturally creates the result I want?

Examples:

Instead of:

“I want to write a book.”

Try:

“I am someone who writes.”

Instead of:

“I want a decluttered home.”

Try:

“I am someone who makes decluttering decisions.”

Instead of:

“I want a YouTube channel.”

Try:

“I am someone who publishes.”

The identity creates direction.

Then Choose One Action

One.

Not five.

Not ten.

One.

Examples:

Writer:

Write for ten minutes.

Publisher:

Publish one piece of content.

Declutterer:

Remove one item.

Reader:

Read five pages.

The action should feel almost too small.

Small actions are easier to repeat.

Repeated actions are what create consistency.

Then Choose One Time

Remove as many decisions as possible.

Instead of:

“When I have time.”

Choose:

  • 7:00 AM
  • 1:00 PM
  • 8:00 PM

Specific times reduce friction.

The fewer decisions required, the easier it becomes to act.

Why Small Actions Work

Small actions look unimpressive.

That is part of their strength.

Many people can maintain:

  • ten minutes
  • one page
  • one item
  • one video
  • one paragraph

Even on difficult days.

Consistency grows from repetition.

Not intensity.

The Re-Entry Rule

This may be the most important part.

Days will be missed.

Sometimes weeks.

That is normal.

The goal is not perfection.

The goal is re-entry.

Missed yesterday?

Continue today.

Missed three days?

Continue today.

Missed two weeks?

Continue today.

The ability to return matters more than the ability to maintain a perfect streak.

Example One: Wild Womaniya

Desired outcome:

A growing collection of useful publications.

Identity:

Someone who experiments, documents, and publishes.

Action:

Work on one publication.

Time:

A chosen publishing session.

The identity guides the action.

The action builds the body of work.

Example Two: The Ecstatic Alchemist

Desired outcome:

Helping people move from knowing to doing.

Identity:

Someone who practices before teaching.

Action:

Run one small experiment.

Time:

A chosen daily practice session.

The method grows through practice.

Not theory.

Example Three: Decluttering

Desired outcome:

A calmer home.

Identity:

Someone who makes small decluttering decisions daily.

Action:

Remove one item.

Time:

Ten minutes after breakfast.

Simple.

Repeatable.

Sustainable.

The Seven-Day Experiment

For the next seven days:

Step 1

Choose one identity.

Step 2

Choose one action.

Step 3

Choose one time.

Step 4

Repeat.

No optimisation.

No redesigning.

No adding extra habits.

Just repeat.

Reflection Questions

  • Is the system simple enough?
  • What part feels unnecessarily complicated?
  • What action could be made smaller?
  • How quickly do I return after missing a day?

Final Thought

Consistency rarely comes from motivation.

It usually comes from simplicity.

One identity.

One action.

One time.

Repeated often enough, simple actions stop feeling like effort.

They start feeling like who you are.

About The Ecstatic Alchemist

At its heart is a simple question:

What becomes possible when we stop waiting and start doing?

The goal isn’t perfection.

It’s progress.
